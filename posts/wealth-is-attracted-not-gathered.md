## The smartest person in the room rarely wins
I've observed something interesting about the wealthy people I know. Most of them are not the smartest people in the room. I've watched successful people make questionable analytical decisions and draw wrong conclusions from basic data. They aren't conventionally intelligent. Yet they're far more successful than people who are.

What they tend to have instead is charisma: an ability to make people feel at ease, to make decisions on instinct, to move fast without overthinking.

When I heard Barbara Corcoran talk about how she was very bad at Maths, it started connecting. Imagine if these people had been the analytical type. They'd probably have been drawn into technical roles, optimizing spreadsheets. They may never have become what they are.

I've always been the analytical type, and I still am. There was a naive period where I actually wished I wasn't, and I wrongly assumed that being less smart somehow led to more wealth. I see people making the same mistake, attributing wealth to single variables like education or IQ. Correlation does not always mean causation.

But I don't shut the idea down entirely either. I like to think of everything in probabilities. These variables might play a role, or they might not. The more you know, the more you realize how little you actually know. Intelligence is not a reliable indicator of wealth. There are millions of variables at play. Perhaps someday we'll build a model that accounts for all of them and truly understands chaos, or even predicts it.

## Your own formula
There is not one path to wealth but many. Everyone has to develop their own formula. We can extract lessons from those who've succeeded, but in the end, it's our own implementation that determines the outcome.

Hard work, the way it's traditionally sold to us, is the biggest scam of our lifetime. Hard work alone doesn't bring wealth. It's your mindset that attracts it. Wealth is attracted, not gathered. It comes to you in ways you cannot fathom. All you have to do is fix your mindset and make room for it.

## Leverage over hours
Most people think about wealth linearly: work more hours, earn more money. That's trading time for money and it has a ceiling. Your time is finite.

The wealthiest people I've studied don't think in terms of hours worked. They think in terms of leverage. Software is leverage. Capital is leverage. A network of trusted people is leverage. One well-placed decision made in an afternoon can outweigh a year of grinding.

The goal isn't to work harder. It's to position yourself where your effort compounds.

## Comfort with uncertainty
There's a relationship between wealth and comfort with not knowing. The people who accumulate real wealth are the ones who can sit with ambiguity. They make decisions with incomplete information and act anyway.

The maths-minded person (and I say this as one) wants to model the situation first. Wants to see the full picture before moving. By the time the picture is clear, the window has closed. Wealth favours speed and conviction over precision and caution.

## Money in motion
Wealthy people think about money differently at a fundamental level. Most people see money as something to hold onto. The wealthy see it as something to move.

Money in motion generates more money. It's counterintuitive: spending can be more productive than saving, if spent in the right places. Investing in people, in relationships, in bets that have asymmetric upside.

The fear of losing money keeps most people poor. The willingness to deploy it keeps others rich.

## Orientation over ability
None of this is to say that skill, discipline, and intelligence don't matter. They do. But they're table stakes. Plenty of skilled, disciplined, intelligent people stay middle class their entire lives.

What separates them from the wealthy isn't ability. It's orientation. Where you point your effort matters more than how much effort you exert. A sharp axe swung at the wrong tree is still wasted energy.
